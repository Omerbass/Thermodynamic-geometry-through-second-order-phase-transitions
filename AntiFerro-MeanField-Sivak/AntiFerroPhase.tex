\subsubsection{Ordered phase}

In this model, as seen in \cite{vivesUnifiedMeanfieldStudy1997}, around the phase transition 
\begin{align}
    m & \approx \pm\sqrt{1-\frac{1}{\beta_c K}} + a_m \Delta \alpha - b_m \Delta \beta \\
    s & \approx \sqrt{a_s \Delta \alpha + b_s \Delta \beta}
\end{align}
With $a,b$ being some positive constants, and 
\begin{align*}
    \Delta \alpha & = \alpha - \alpha_c \\
    \Delta \beta & = \beta - \beta_c
\end{align*}
For shorter notation we'll denote 
\begin{align*}
    \Delta_m & = a_m \Delta \alpha - b_m \Delta \beta \\
    \Delta_s & = a_s \Delta \alpha + b_s \Delta \beta
\end{align*}

One can show that to lowest order in $\Delta$
\begin{align}
    m_{A,B} & \approx \pm\sqrt{1-\frac{1}{\beta_c K}} \pm \sqrt{\Delta_s} \\
    \zeta_{A,B} & \approx \frac{1}{\beta_c K} \left(1 \pm 2\sqrt{\Delta_s}\right)
\end{align}

And the metric is to lowest order proportional to $\Delta_s^{-1}$. Meaning it diverges, but from the same considerations as in chapter \ref{subsec:ScalingHypothesisResults}, the length remains finite.
